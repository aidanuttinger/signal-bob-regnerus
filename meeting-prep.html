Likely Questions — Have These Ready
"How long did this take you to build?"
Don't answer with hours. "The right amount of time to get it right." What matters is what you're holding — nine deliverables, a website, a working tool. The time it took is irrelevant to the value.
"Can I share this with my team / send it to someone?"
Yes — that's the URL. It's live and shareable. This is actually a good signal — if he wants to share it, he values it. Note who he wants to share it with.
"What happens next? How do we actually implement this?"
This is the opening. "That's exactly the right question." Then present the engagement options. The session was the diagnosis. Implementation is the engagement.
"The SPARK archetypes — did you make these up?"
Honest answer: "We synthesized them from your session language — they're not pulled from your existing work. They belong to you if they're right. You tell us if they reflect what you actually see in your clients."
"How is this different from a regular strategy session?"
"Most strategy sessions produce a PDF that lives in a folder. This lives on the internet, works as a lead magnet, and is already a website redesign. The session wasn't just analysis — it produced assets."
Watch-Outs
⚠️
Don't overwhelm him with all 9 chapters. Show him the intro, the hub scope, Chapter 06, the bonuses, and the code. Let him explore the rest on his own. Too much in one meeting = decision fatigue.
⚠️
Don't over-explain the SPARK archetypes. Flag clearly that they need his validation. Don't defend them — present them as a draft for his input. His ownership of them matters more than being right.
⚠️
Don't skip the code moment. It's tempting to rush to next steps. The code is the emotional close of the session — it's where Bob feels that this was built for him specifically. Give it space.
⚠️
Don't pitch before he asks. Let the session speak for itself for the first 30 minutes. The close comes naturally after the code reveal — not before.
